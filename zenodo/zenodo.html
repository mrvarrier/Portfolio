UX Case Study - Zenodo Integration for Correlate

TEAM AND ROLE

As the UX Designer on this project, I collaborated with a cross-functional team including two developers and a product manager. My responsibilities encompassed conducting user research, creating wireframes and prototypes, usability testing, and ensuring the design aligned with both user needs and technical requirements. I also worked closely with the development team to ensure technical feasibility while maintaining a focus on user experience.

TIME FRAME

7 weeks (February 2024 - April 2024)

OVERVIEW

A seamless integration enabling EU project partners to archive their research data from Correlate to Zenodo.

The Zenodo integration allows Correlate users, particularly those involved in EU Horizon 2020 projects, to archive their research data in compliance with open-source data preservation requirements. Through this integration, users can select documents and data they've created in Correlate and publish them to Zenodo—a trusted open-access repository—without leaving the Correlate platform. This solution provides necessary metadata management and ensures long-term accessibility of research outputs beyond the project lifecycle.

CONTEXT

EU-funded research projects require long-term archiving solutions for sustainability and compliance.

Correlate serves as a collaboration platform for several EU Horizon 2020 projects, with a commitment to deliver data archiving functionality by April 2024. While Correlate excels at facilitating real-time collaboration, the platform doesn't inherently support long-term data preservation—a critical requirement for EU-funded research. EU project partners must store their research outputs in approved open-source repositories like Zenodo at the end of their projects to ensure public accessibility and compliance with open science principles. This integration represents both a contractual obligation and a strategic opportunity for Correlate to provide added value for academic and research users.

RESEARCH

Technical exploration of Zenodo API capabilities

Before diving into user requirements, I worked with our development team to thoroughly explore the Zenodo API documentation and understand its technical capabilities and limitations. We needed to establish what was technically possible before defining our integration approach. This research determined key metadata requirements, authentication processes, and file handling constraints that would influence our design decisions.

User interviews with EU project stakeholders

I conducted interviews with Work Package Leaders and Task Leaders from our two key EU project partners to understand their specific archiving needs, current workflows, and expectations for the integration. These conversations were crucial in identifying that many researchers were unfamiliar with archiving processes and would need clear guidance through the metadata creation steps.

Competitive analysis of existing archiving solutions

I examined how other platforms approach archiving workflows and integrations with repositories. This revealed that most solutions either required users to navigate away from their primary platform or provided overly complex interfaces with too many options, creating confusion and abandonment of the archiving process.

INSIGHT #1

Our research revealed a significant tension between comprehensive metadata collection and user efficiency. EU researchers frequently have back-to-back meetings and limited time to complete administrative tasks like archiving. While proper archiving requires thorough metadata, researchers strongly preferred a streamlined process that prioritized speed and simplicity over exhaustive options.

INSIGHT #2

We discovered a critical user concern regarding permanence. Many researchers were unaware that once documents are published to Zenodo, they cannot be deleted—only new versions can be added. This highlighted the need for clear communication about the permanence of archiving actions and the option to save drafts before committing to publication.

INSIGHT #3

Technical research revealed a fundamental challenge: Correlate stores links to documents rather than the documents themselves. Unlike typical integrations where files are directly transferred, we would need to temporarily download files to our server before uploading them to Zenodo—a technical constraint that would significantly impact our design approach and user experience.

PROBLEM STATEMENT

"How might we create a seamless archiving experience that enables EU project partners to efficiently transfer their Correlate documents to Zenodo with appropriate metadata, while accommodating technical constraints and ensuring clarity about permanent publishing actions?"

IDEATION & DESIGN

Two Competing Approaches to Archiving

Our initial design challenge centered on how users would access the archiving functionality. We developed two distinct approaches: a file-centric model where users would select individual files for archiving directly from their boards, and a dedicated "My Archive" space that would serve as a central hub for organizing and preparing documents for archiving. The latter approach would require significant changes to Correlate's information architecture but offered more flexibility for bulk operations.

Simplifying Technical Complexity

One of our biggest challenges was how to handle the technical reality that Correlate doesn't store files directly but only links to them. Our initial solution required a complex flow of temporarily downloading files to our server before uploading to Zenodo. This created concerns about server capacity and potential download failures. After several iterations and technical discussions, we simplified the approach by focusing initially on entire Correlate boards rather than individual files, reducing the complexity of file handling while still meeting user needs.

Metadata Collection: Finding the Balance

The most challenging aspect of the design was creating an interface for collecting necessary metadata without overwhelming users. Zenodo requires specific fields for publication, but our research indicated users wanted minimal form-filling. I designed a progressive disclosure approach where only the five mandatory fields (Resource Type, Title, Publication Date, Creators, and Access Rights) would be shown by default, with clear visual separation between required and optional fields. We also implemented intelligent defaults where possible—for example, auto-populating the title from the board name and extracting creator information from the user profile.

Critical Design Pivot: Draft vs. Publish

Our initial design assumed users would want to directly publish to Zenodo. However, through usability testing, we discovered significant user anxiety about committing to permanent publication without review. This led to our most important design change: prominently featuring both "Save as Draft" and "Publish" options, with Draft as the default action. This single change dramatically reduced user hesitation and improved confidence in the archiving process.

Streamlined User Flow

For our MVP, we designed a three-step process: 1) Select content to archive, 2) Input required metadata with smart defaults, 3) Choose between saving as draft or publishing directly. Each step included clear guidance about the implications of user actions, particularly regarding the permanence of publishing. The final design clearly communicated that drafts could be edited later on Zenodo, while publications would be permanent but could have new versions added.

Authentication and Integration Challenges

A significant technical and design challenge was how to handle Zenodo authentication. We initially considered a deeply integrated approach where users would authorize Correlate to publish on their behalf. However, this raised concerns about permission management and created a complex technical implementation. After several iterations and discussions with stakeholders, we simplified the approach by having users authenticate directly with Zenodo at the point of archiving. While this created an additional step, it provided users with greater clarity about which account they were publishing to and simplified the technical implementation for our April 1st deadline.

RESULTS AND FUTURE WORK

Our project earned significant recognition, winning first place overall and the "Best Life Hack" award at HackHarvard. Additionally, Empath.ly was selected as one of the top 80 startups among more than 1,500 competitors in India, validating our innovative approach to solving remote communication challenges.

This intensive hackathon experience greatly advanced my skills as a UX designer. I gained valuable expertise in data visualization techniques and deepened my understanding of how different user contexts impact design decisions. Working under the extreme time constraint of 36 hours taught me how to prioritize effectively and deliver high-quality work under pressure.

Our key insight for future development is that creating a standalone application isn't optimal. Users already have established workflows with platforms like Zoom and Google Meet. Rather than asking them to adopt an entirely new tool, our next step is to develop Empath.ly as a plugin for existing platforms, starting with Zoom. This approach will allow us to deliver our emotional analytics capabilities within the tools users already rely on daily.
